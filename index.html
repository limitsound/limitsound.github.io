Frequently asked questions

How do I limit the volume of AirPods on a Mac?

macOS has no built-in Headphone Safety like the iPhone. LimitSound adds it: pick a dB ceiling in the menu bar and the system volume is capped whenever your AirPods connect — raise it and it snaps back instantly.

Does it work with AirPods Pro and AirPods Max?

Yes — every AirPods generation (including renamed ones like "Alex's AirPods Pro") is detected automatically.

Does it work with Sony, Bose, JBL and other Bluetooth headphones?

Yes. Enable "Protect all Bluetooth headphones" in the panel and any Bluetooth audio device is covered, not just AirPods.

Can I use it to limit the volume on my kid's MacBook?

Yes — set the dB ceiling and enable "Launch at login" so the limit is active after every restart. Note the setting isn't password-protected yet, so a determined teenager can change it; it's designed as protection, not parental lockdown.

What dB limit should I choose?

Hearing organizations commonly cite ~80–85 dB as a reasonable ceiling for extended listening. Most people pick 85 dB. This isn't medical advice — see our terms for the honest details on accuracy.

Why does macOS warn me when installing?

LimitSound is distributed outside the App Store and isn't yet registered with Apple's paid developer program, so the first launch needs System Settings → Privacy & Security → "Open Anyway" — once. Updates install with one click after that.

Honest fine print

macOS doesn't expose the true sound-pressure level of headphones to third-party apps — only Apple can do that. LimitSound's dB scale is a calibrated approximation anchored at ≈100 dB for full volume: the 5 dB steps are accurate relative to each other, while the absolute level depends on your headphone model, the track, and fit. It's a strong safety net, not a lab instrument.
